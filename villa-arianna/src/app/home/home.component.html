Visual Preservation at Roman Stabiae is a web database to accompany a forthcoming book of the same title by the Stabiae Archaeological Team of the University of Maryland Architecture Program.

Built in the late first century BCE and buried by the eruption of Vesuvius in 79 CE (the same disaster that befell nearby Pompeii and Herculaneum), the Villa Arianna comprises almost 100 rooms and represents the height of elite villa culture in the Late Republic and Early Empire. The fossilized remains of the villa are precious evidence for early Roman painting techniques, formations of interior and exterior space (including a peristyle garden as large as any in the Roman world), private rituals, and representations of nature. Unfortunately, the villa’s position on a cliff makes it vulnerable to erosion from storms and settling. Thus the great terrace supporting the suite of ‘panoramic rooms’ facing Vesuvius is mostly lost. As the Arianna continues to deteriorate, it is still missing a detailed illustrated survey of itsfrescoed rooms equal to recent efforts at the Villa San Marco in Stabiae, the villas of Oplontis, and the houses of Pompeii.

This interactive site exhibits our archaeological illustrations and photographs of the surviving walls in the excavated core of the villa that contain significant traces of Roman fresco. The photographs are an efficient medium for conveying the appearance of the frescoed walls from a viewer’s perspective, capturing color and spatial depth. Meanwhile the illustrations (digital line-drawn elevations) constitute a robust visual record of the walls in their current state, accurately representing the dimensions, elevation levels, proportions, and physical diversity of the complex wall surfaces, which preserve not only fresco but also exposed masonry and archaeological signatures expressing various historical processes. Our innovative recording methodology, which integrates on-site observation, hand-drawing, and historical learning with automated measuring techniques (EDM, LiDAR, and photogrammetry), presents a new standard of accuracy and detail in the visual preservation of Roman decorated houses.

You are invited to explore these images through browse and search options, or to navigate the plan of the villa for specific rooms. Each room has a dedicated page with a brief description, images and archaeological illustrations of each frescoed wall, and information about its subject matter, painting style, materials, and room function. Each room is also tagged with this information to facilitate search queries. Through this accessible format, we hope to offer new pathways into this rich archaeological site.
